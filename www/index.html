What matters to you when choosing treatments for knee osteoarthritis?

There are many treatment options available for the management of knee osteoarthritis.

Some of them are highly effective, reducing pain and improving joint function and overall quality of life; others have limited or unclear effect on pain or disability. Some are widely available, easily accessible, and affordable for all people with osteoarthritis; others are expensive or involve long travel or waiting times to receive treatment. Some are very safe, with few or no side-effects and little risk of harm; others may have potentially severe complications.

Understanding how consumers, healthcare providers, health policy makers, and other stakeholders weigh up and trade off these different attributes of treatments is crucial to ensure good value, effective, accessible, acceptable, and equitable care can be made available to all people with osteoarthritis.

This tool presents the results of a study designed to elicit from key stakeholders the attributes of treatment considered most important in making decisions about osteoarthritis care and their preferences for trade-offs among these attributes; to describe the performance of osteoarthritis interventions against the attributes, informed by high-quality clinical practice guidelines and systematic reviews of peer-reviewed research; and to prioritise interventions by assessing their performance relative to stakeholder or individual preferences.

How it works

In this application, interventions for the clinical management of knee osteoarthritis are characterised according to eight attributes: recommendation (appropriateness for the stage of the disease), quality of evidence, cost, duration of effect, accessibility, risk of mild/moderate harm, risk of serious harm, and effectiveness. Each attribute describes between 3 and 5 levels of performance, in increasing order of desirability (for the details of the levels described by each attribute, click the help icon next to the attribute heading in the Preference Weights panel).

Evidence:

In our study, the performance of each intervention according to these eight attributes was informed by the Royal Australian College of General Practitioners' Guideline for the management of knee and hip osteoarthritis (2017), supplemented where necessary by additional sources including (in decreasing order of preference) systematic reviews and meta-analyses, other published data, and research team judgement.

The performance scoring for each intervention can differ at different stages of the disease course (for example, total joint replacement has little evidence and is generally not recommended/appropriate for early-stage disease, but is strongly recommended for patients with severe late-stage disease); to incorporate this in the ranking model, evidence tables can be constructed for early, mid, or late-stage disease using the 'Timing of interventions' option.

In future iterations of the evidence table, we will also incorporate the option to select different sources of evidence to inform the table and resulting preference scores. (At this stage, only the Royal Australian College of General Practitioners' Guideline for the management of knee and hip osteoarthritis (2017) is available.)

Interventions

A total of 74 interventions are included in the current version of the evidence table. These can be filtered by category and, if required, by the selection of specific interventions in the Interventions option panel.

Preference Weights

Interventions are ranked by summing up their total "preference weights", or values associated with the performance of the intervention on each of the attributes, using weights calculated from the derived preferences of key stakeholders in our study. These can be changed if desired, using the sliders/numeric inputs in the Preference Weights panel, to investigate the resulting effects on the intervetion ranking.

To avoid potential biases in stakeholder evaluations of the interventions due to prior beliefs or pre-conceived ideas about different treatments, preference weights, or the relative values assigned to each intervention, were derived indirectly by obtaining preference weights for the eight attributes, which can then be summed to derive an aggregate preference score for each intervention. The preference weights presented here were derived from a study of the preferences of key New Zealand stakeholder groups (consumers with OA, Māori health advocates, health care providers, health policy makers, and content area experts). To investigate the effect on preference rankings using any other set of preference weights, move the sliders/numeric inputs in the Preference Weights panel.

Outputs

To switch between the preference plot and viewing the underlying evidence table, select the Plot or Evidence Table tabs.

The plot shows the aggregate preference score for each selected intervention, and the contribution of each attribute level to this total. You can click on the bar for an intervention to provide a summary of the attribute levels and associated preference weights for that intervention. Click elsewhere on the plot to remove the summary.

The evidence table reports the performance of each selected intervention on the eight attributes, according to the specified evidence source and timing of interventions, and the resulting aggregate preference score using the specified preference weights. Interventions are (by default) ranked by total preference score. The table can be sorted by intervention name, attribute, or preference score by clicking on the column headers, and searched for specific interventions or attribute levels using the search box

Further details of the study, including recruitment of key stakeholders, theoretical basis for the preference-elicitation study, and construction of the evidence tables, can be found here.

Timing of Interventions

Some interventions are considered more appropriate or have higher quality evidence at different stages of the disease course. In this study, we considered the appropriateness of each intervention for early/mild, mid/moderate, and late/severe osteoarthritis, corresponding approximately to their recommendation as first-line (core long-term management), second-line (optional adjunctive management), or third-line (surgical management for end-stage osteoarthritis) treatment options.


Options:
  • Overall: the evidence table is based on the overall recommendation and data from the Clinical Practice Guidelines
  • Early/Mid/Late: the recommendation attribute is adjusted based on whether interventions were considered as core, optional adjunctive, or end-stage surgical management treatment options

Further details of the adjustments for timing of interventions will be provided in upcoming publications.

Source of Evidence

Different sources of evidence (e.g clinical practice guidelines, systematic reviews) can be selected (at this stage, only the RACGP guidelines are available).


RACGP Guideline:
Royal Australian College of General Practitioners' Guideline for the management of knee and hip osteoarthritis (2017)

Interventions

Interventions can be filtered by type using these check boxes, and if desired further filtered by specific intervention in the filter selection box below.


If no filter is applied, all interventions (of the selected types) will be shown.

Preference Weights

The attributes are aggregated, by default, using the New Zealand stakeholder preference weights elicited as part of our project 'The impact and management of rising osteoarthritis burden'.

If you want to use alternative preference weights, these can be set using the sliders or numeric input boxes in this section.

All preference weights must sum to 100. This is done automatically for the table and plot; to see the recalculated values in the input fields, use the 'Normalize Weights' button.

Weights can be reset to the NZ stakeholder weights using the 'Reset' button

Recommendation for using the treatment at the given stage of OA

Providing or using the right treatments or services at the early/mild, mid, or late/advanced stage of OA. For example, it would not be recommended or appropriate to use powerful drug treatments such as opioids before, say, self-management and education, physical exercise, or less-powerful drug therapies such as paracetamol.


Levels (best to worst):
  • Strong for: all or almost all informed people would use the treatment at this stage
  • Conditional for: most informed people would use the treatment at this stage, but not all
  • Neutral
  • Conditional against: most informed people would try another treatment at this stage, but not all
  • Strong against: all or almost all informed people would try another treatment at this stage

Note: ...but not all means that a substantial number of people would still choose (or not choose) the treatment

Quality of the evidence – how confident you are that the treatment works

The extent to which one can be confident that the effects of the treatment or service described are real. 'Evidence' can mean different things to different people, however, there is an accepted hierarchy of how valid each source is. For example, anecdotal claims about the effectiveness of treatment in advertisements, from peers or family members, or from individual treatment providers may not be as convincing as independent health professional advice, rigorous research, peer-reviewed Systematic Reviews, or authoritative Clinical Practice Guidelines.


Levels (best to worst):
  • High: further research is very unlikely to change our confidence in the likely effect of the intervention
  • Moderate: further research is likely to have an important impact on our confidence in the likelihood of effect of the intervention and may change the estimate
  • Low: further research is very likely to have an important impact on our confidence in the likelihood of effect of the intervention and is likely to change the estimate
  • Very low: Any estimate of the treatment effect is very uncertain

Cost of Treatment

Total financial costs relevant to the use or provision of health care for OA - e.g. costs to the health system, out-of-pocket costs to the consumer and, the societal costs of providing health care for OA. Societal costs include tax revenue and lost wages due to time away from work, reduced employment or early retirement.


Levels (best to worst):
  • Low: $0-$100 per month OR less than $1500 total
  • Medium: $100-$1000 per month OR $1500-$15,000 total
  • High: $1000 or more per month OR $15,000+ total

Duration – how long the treatment effect lasts

The length of time the benefits of the treatment last. E.g., the beneficial effects of surgery, if appropriate, may last for 10-15 years after initial healing has occurred, with little ongoing care until 10-15 years have elapsed. In contrast, drug therapy may require frequent dosing every 4 hours to maintain its effect on pain.


Levels (best to worst):
  • Long: the effects of the treatment are experienced for 10 years or longer
  • Medium: the effects of the treatment are experienced for several months to several years
  • Short: the effects of the treatment are experienced for up to 4-6 hours

Accessibility

The extent to which the treatment or service can be accessed by people with OA. For example, the distance to nearest provider, wait time and, the ability for cultural and linguistic groups or people from diverse sociodemographic backgrounds to equally access health care for OA (fairness).


Levels (best to worst):
  • Accessible: the treatment can be accessed by the person living with OA in a week or so, regardless of their travel needs
  • Neither accessible nor inaccessible
  • Inaccessible: There may be a waiting time of a month or more to receive the treatment; the provider may be inconvenient to reach; or the treatment may not be accessible at all because of health system-related factors

Risk of mild or moderate side-effects

Mild to moderate treatment side-effects associated with comfort or safety - e.g. temporary pain, discomfort, nausea, heartburn or stomach pain.


Levels (best to worst):
  • Low: 1 in 4 chance (25%)
  • Moderate: 2 in 4 chance (50%)
  • High: 3 in 4 chance (75%)

Risk of serious harm

Severe treatment side-effects - e.g. implant failure, drug toxicity, stomach bleeding or ulcer.


Levels (best to worst):
  • Low: 1 in 500 chance (0.2%)
  • Moderate: 1 in 200 chance (0.5%)
  • High: 1 in 50 chance (2%)

Effectiveness

The ability for the treatment or service to achieve the desired result - e.g. the change in pain and function, caused by the intervention.

Effectiveness is different to quality of the evidence because it describes the impact, or how ‘big’ the change caused by the treatment is, not how likely it is to happen, or how confident you are that it’ll happen – this is the 'quality of the evidence'. For example, a highly effective treatment with a very low quality of evidence means that the likelihood, or chance of it actually working is very small but, if it did work, it would have a high/large impact on pain and/or function.

Effectiveness is measured by the standardised mean difference (SMD) in pain levels, defined as the mean reduction in pain levels following treatment, divided by the standard deviation of baseline pain levels.


Levels (best to worst):
  • High: SMD > 0.5
  • Medium: 0.2 < SMD < 0.5
  • Low: SMD < 0.2
